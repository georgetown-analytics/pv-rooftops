Overview of the Solar Capstone Project

The Solar Domain
Photovoltaic solar cell technology is a rapidly advancing source of renewable, inexhaustible, and clean energy for utilities, businesses, and households. Among alternative energy technologies, the rooftop photovoltaic (PV) system is one of most popular and increasingly attractive technologies available for residential use. Over the past decade, driven by the rapid development of revolutionary PV technologies and drops in overall installation price, rooftop PV installations have experienced exponential growth in the US and around the world. The market is projected to continue its rapid growth, driven by continued improvements in PV cell efficiency, installation cost improvements, and society’s concerns about the detrimental impact of carbon release associated with traditional energy generation.


Motivation
Our team aims to construct a model to predict the market size for residential PV rooftop technology in a select group of metro areas based on user-provided variables, such as installation costs, as well as general market conditions within each metro area, and thereby provide strategic forecasting to assist PV rooftop system manufacturers, installers, policy-makers, and utility companies.


Initial Hypothesis
In our initial hypothesis, we suggest the volume of rooftop PV solar installations within each metro area is affected by several key market factors, including housing type populations in each metro area (numbers of single families, townhouses, commercial, etc), household income, party registration, local electricity rates, education level, the availability and reviews of local installation contractors, installation price, etc.


Why?
While less than 1% of total 2015 energy production in the United States was attributable to solar energy, the pace of technological advancement and promise of a clean, inexhaustible power source have made it one of the best hopes for sustaining the US’s high living standards and avoiding the potentially disastrous consequences of the carbon-based economy.

However, the infrastructure investments and supporting policies necessary for managing the intermittent power source and enabling local utilities to handle the decentralized nature of grid-connected PV rooftop systems are not insignificant.

Accurately forecasting the number of future PV rooftop installations is critical to the timing and coordination of these policies and investments. Also, businesses interested in supporting PV rooftop system installations also desire to time their entry into new markets to be in step with potential demand and ahead of their rivals in meeting that demand.

Rapid technological developments and the associated fall in PV rooftop installation costs means that the solar industry may be approaching a potential tipping point in several markets that could catch business and government policy-makers flat-footed without the right forecasting tools. Our capstone project offers policy-makers, business, and other interested parties an interactive tool for inputting simple variables, such as price, county, and/or year, and making predictions about the future demand for PV rooftop systems.

Why?

  • While solar energy makes up only ~1% of total electrical generation, it is the fastest growing source of electrical generating capacity in the US.

  • Federal, state, and local governments, as well as utility companies have to plan for potentially massive infrastructure investments necessary to effectively manage this distributed and intermittent source of electricity.

  • The installation cost per watt for residential PV rooftop systems is quickly approaching grid parity, the economic breakeven point, beyond which consumers can capture a positive ROI from installing a PV rooftop system. Model results suggest that there is a grid-to-installation price tipping point beyond which exponential growth can be anticipated.

  • Businesses armed with good predictive models are posed to capture profits early from burgeoning demand in ripening markets and enjoy the benefits of a first-mover advantage while minimizing the risk of entering too early.

Data Collection Approach

  • The team drafted a statistical abstract based on the economic theory of demand as the basis for its data collection strategy.

  • According to economic theory, key determinants of demand are:
    • Product price
    • Availability / price of complementary goods
    • Price of substitute goods
    • Income / income distribution
    • Consumer preferences
    • Government policy

  • Using the general economic determinants of demand as a guide, the team went about identifying government and other data sources which they believed measured determinants of demand specific to PV rooftop systems.

Data Sources

  • Tracking the Sun Public Data File: a data effort by the Lawrence Berkley National Laboratory to capture key data from nation-wide PV installs.
  • American Community Survey data: the US Census Bureau performs regular statistical samples of the US population to capture and measure key demographic characteristics, such as income, housing stock
  • Energy Information Agency: a government agency responsible for capturing and reporting key data about the US energy industry, such as the average retail price of electricity by year and state
  • County-level Presidential General Election results 2012: the data, compiled and reported by The Guardian, provides raw vote counts for candidates on the ballot in each county.
  • Freeing the Grid grades: The Interstate Renewable Energy Council (IREC)’s annually assesses state policy and regulations on a A-F scale with respect to net metering and grid interconnectivity.

Data Wrangling

  • Based on the available data, the team used quarterly installation counts by county as its instances for modeling PV rooftop demand.
  • Features were not all available or had perceived measurement issues at the county-level but had to be applied appropriately to that level of detail. For instance:
    • Price per installed watt was estimated nationally by quarter due to perceived outlier / small sample size issues
    • Income / housing stock / retail electricity prices / Presidential election results were not available quarterly, and due to the relatively stable nature of these features, the team used 2015 data across the entire feature set. (Exception: Presidential election results were from 2012.)
    • Retail electricity prices and net metering / interconnectivity policy grades were only available at the state level and were applied at the state-level across the feature set.
  • The team performed multiple SQL queries to produce joinable table views at the appropriate level of detail and then in stepwise fashion joined the instances and features into a consolidated table view for machine learning.

Machine Learning

  • The team then proceeded to pipe the initial instances/features table into the scikit-learn available machine learning algorithms.

  • The linear regression models based on the initial dataset show low levels of explanatory power (R-squared < 0.3), whereas non-linear regressions show very high levels of explanatory power (R-squared > 0.9)

  • However, the linear regression results gave the team pause (given the economic theory of demand’s empirical strength) and encouraged them to perform feature transformations that might improve the linear regression results.

  • With feature transformations such as dividing the retail price of electricity by the price per installed watt, taking the exponent of that result, and multiplying it by the relevant housing stock, the team was able to improve the R-squared of the linear regressions from <0.3 to 0.6 – 0.73.

  • While the transformed dataset did not materially impact the non-linear results, it gave the team more confidence in their soundness.

Cross Validation

  • Decision Tree: 0.55
  • Random Forest: 0.63
  • K-neighbors: 0.46
Visual inspection of the model results suggest lower levels of heteroscedasticity in the Random Forest regression model

Cross-validation results support Random Forest model as the superior predictor of residential PV rooftop installations and allayed (somewhat) the team’s concerns about overfitting.

Data Product API and UI

To predict future quarterly installation counts per county, the pickled random forest regression model requests the values of the following 12 features as inputs:
  • number of single-family households
  • number of attached households
  • number of total households
  • number of households that have an income between 75k and 100k
  • number of households that has an income between 100k and 150k
  • number of households that has an income between 75k and 100k
  • number of households that has an income above 200k
  • Democratic share*(number of single_family houses + attached houses)
  • exp(interconnectivity) * (number of single_family houses + attached houses)
  • exp(net_metering) * (number of single_family households + attached houses)
  • Feature X11: exp(grid_to_panel) * (number of single_family housesholds + attached houses)
  • latitude

The final data product comprises 3 main steps:
  • The installation price is requested as an input and the value for feature X11 “exp(grid_to_panel) * (sfh + th)” is then calculated automatically
  • Feature X11 along with the most recent values (i.e. values from year 2015) of the other 11 features get plugged into the pickled random forest model for predictions.
  • A choropleth map at the county level is then generated as the data product’s visual output.

Future Work

The team’s first recommendation towards improving the data product would be to improve the underlying predictive model. As discussed previously, the team explored a number of machine learning algorithms in building the predictive model it selected, and the team picked the best performing model – a random forest regression model. Although the model achieved a high R square score (0.95) and achieved good, if not great, cross-validation performance scores, it does not perform as well in estimating values at the extremes (with a problem of underestimating high values)

To accomplish this, the team would ingest and incorporate other datasets to study their impacts on the predictive model. Potential datasets worth exploring would include county level tree canopy data, previous install bases of PV system, and Yelp review data for local PV rooftop installers. Another avenue worthy of pursuit would be to transform the label data (independent variables) to make it more centered. This effort might improve the model’s performance on predicting values at the extremes.

The data product’s secondary model for predicting installation price over time was trained using national level data. To improve this model, we would potentially ingest and incorporate county level data to enable a more localized prediction of installed price per watt. The team would also consider incorporating other datasets that affect the installation price temporally, such as variables that predict the advancement of PV technology or track utility / local government incentives / rebates.

    Why is the model only dependent on price?
    • Our machine learning model uses a combination of 12 different features in order to predict household solar rooftop installs for each U.S. county. Our team used the randomized lasso algorithm to evaluate the importance of all 12 features. Results show that the "Exp(grid_to_panel) * (sfh + th)" feature has the highest feature score. This feature is calculated as the exponent of the ratio of the relevant electricity price to installation price multiplied by the sum of single-family homes and attached houses for each county in the United States. Theoretically, the electricity price and the quantity of households in the U.S. should stay relatively stable over the short- to mid-term, while the installation price for a rooftop solar system is expected to dramatically decrease. (For instance, the price dropped at an average rate of 9% per year over the last 8 years). Additionally, the values of the other 11 features are also considered relatively stable over time. Therefore, for this first data webapp, the team considers Price (i.e. solar rooftop installation price per watt) as the key factor that affects predicted solar rooftop installs per county, and therefore, it is the value we request from the user when generating a new model.
